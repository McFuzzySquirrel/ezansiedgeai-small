# eZansiEdgeAI — User Personas (V1)

> Five primary personas that represent the people V1 must serve. Every feature decision should be tested against at least one of these personas with the question: *"Does this make their life better without adding cost, complexity, or risk?"*

---

## 1. Thandiwe — Grade 6 Learner (Rural School)

### Context & Background
Thandiwe is 12 years old and attends a no-fee primary school in a village outside Mthatha, Eastern Cape. Her class has 48 learners and one teacher who covers all subjects. She walks 3 km to school. At home she lives with her grandmother; her mother works in Durban and sends money monthly. Thandiwe is bright and curious but struggles with fractions and geometry because the classroom pace leaves no room for re-explanation.

### Device
- **Phone:** Mobicel Hero (hand-me-down from her mother) — Android 10, 2 GB RAM, 32 GB storage (8 GB free).
- **Condition:** Cracked screen (bottom-right corner), battery health ~70%.

### Connectivity
- No home Wi-Fi. No school Wi-Fi. Family SIM has a R29 weekly data bundle (≈ 200 MB) shared with grandmother for WhatsApp.
- Nearest free Wi-Fi: town library, 12 km away.

### Goals
- Understand fractions well enough to keep up in class.
- Feel less embarrassed when the teacher asks questions she can't answer.
- Use her phone for something educational (grandmother approves of this; disapproves of TikTok).

### Pain Points
- Data is too expensive to watch educational YouTube videos.
- Khan Academy requires persistent internet.
- Existing "edu apps" are heavy downloads (200+ MB) and require accounts she can't create (no email).
- Phone overheats and battery dies quickly when running heavy apps.

### What Success Looks Like for Thandiwe
She opens the app during the 30 minutes of evening light before load-shedding, reads through the fractions topic at her own pace, picks an explanation style that uses cooking examples (familiar context), tries three practice problems, and closes the app — all without using any data. The phone's battery drops by 1%.

## 2. Sipho — Grade 6 Learner (Township School)

### Context & Background
Sipho is 11 and lives in Khayelitsha, Cape Town. He attends a Section 21 primary school (government-funded, community-governed). His parents both work — his father is a taxi driver, his mother works at a packaging factory. Sipho has two younger siblings. He is good at mental arithmetic but finds word problems and data handling confusing because the English phrasing trips him up (isiXhosa is his home language).

### Device
- **Phone:** Xiaomi Redmi 10C — Android 12, 4 GB RAM, 64 GB storage (22 GB free).
- **Condition:** Good. Screen protector cracked, but display intact. Shared with his younger sister in the evenings.

### Connectivity
- Intermittent 4G at home (coverage drops during peak hours).
- Family has a R99/month 1 GB data plan. Sipho is allowed limited data use for schoolwork.
- School has Wi-Fi but it's locked to the admin office.

### Goals
- Get better at word problems so he can score higher in the June exam.
- Have a way to practise maths when his sister isn't using the phone.
- Feel confident enough to help his younger siblings with their homework.

### Pain Points
- Word problems are written in English he doesn't fully understand; he needs simpler phrasing or isiXhosa scaffolding.
- Shared device means his progress might get mixed up with his sister's usage.
- He's reluctant to ask the teacher for help in front of 43 classmates.

### What Success Looks Like for Sipho
He selects his profile from a simple name list (no password), chooses "simple English" as his reading level, and works through data-handling exercises with visual bar graphs. He switches to his sister's profile when she wants a turn. Neither of them uses any data. The next day he volunteers an answer in class because he recognises the problem pattern.

## 3. Ms. Dlamini — Grade 6 Mathematics Teacher

### Context & Background
Nompumelelo Dlamini (34) teaches Grade 5 and Grade 6 Maths and Natural Sciences at a combined primary school in Soweto, Gauteng. She has 42 learners in her Grade 6 class. She completed her B.Ed at the University of Johannesburg and has 8 years of teaching experience. She is passionate but stretched thin — she also coaches netball and serves on the school governing body.

### Device
- **Personal phone:** Samsung Galaxy A14 — Android 13, 4 GB RAM, 64 GB storage.
- **School resources:** One desktop PC (Windows 7) in the staff room, shared among 11 teachers. No projector. No tablets for learners.

### Connectivity
- Personal phone has a 2 GB monthly data plan.
- School has a 10 Mbps fibre line but Wi-Fi covers only the admin block.

### Goals
- Find a tool that helps her weaker learners catch up without requiring her to give one-on-one time she doesn't have.
- Align supplementary resources to the CAPS pacing schedule so she doesn't have to "translate" between the app and her lesson plan.
- Distribute the tool to learners without needing to involve the school's (non-existent) IT support.

### Pain Points
- She has tried recommending apps before, but learners couldn't install them (account walls, data requirements, incompatible devices).
- She has no time to learn complex admin dashboards or create digital content.
- She worries about recommending anything that could expose learners to inappropriate content or privacy risks.
- Past ed-tech initiatives at her school failed because they required infrastructure (tablets, routers) that broke within months.

### What Success Looks Like for Ms. Dlamini
She receives a single APK file at a district workshop. During break, she installs it on her phone in 2 minutes. She transfers it to 5 learners' phones via Bluetooth. She tells the learners: "Open the app and go to Term 2 → Fractions." No accounts, no passwords, no configuration. The following week, two of the five learners answer fractions questions correctly for the first time.

## 4. Mr. Mokoena — School Administrator / Principal

### Context & Background
Thabo Mokoena (51) is the principal of a rural combined school (Grade R–7) in Limpopo with 380 learners and 12 teachers. He manages the school's NSNP feeding scheme, LTSM (learning materials) budget, infrastructure maintenance, and reporting to the circuit office. He has basic computer literacy — he uses email, Word, and the SA-SAMS system.

### Device
- **Personal phone:** Samsung Galaxy A04 — Android 12, 3 GB RAM, 32 GB storage.
- **School resources:** Two desktops (both running Windows 10, one unreliable). A printer that works intermittently. No server, no network, no IT contract.

### Connectivity
- School has no internet connection. The district office occasionally provides a dongle with 5 GB of data for SA-SAMS uploads.
- Personal data: R149/month for 2 GB.

### Goals
- Approve tools that help learners without adding line items to his non-existent IT budget.
- Avoid any solution that requires him to troubleshoot technology, negotiate with vendors, or sign data-processing agreements.
- Demonstrate to the circuit manager that his school is adopting innovation (this helps with discretionary funding).

### Pain Points
- Every ed-tech vendor he has spoken to requires internet, servers, or per-learner licensing fees.
- He cannot maintain any system that needs updates, patches, or admin passwords.
- He is personally liable under POPIA if a tool he endorses leaks learner data.
- Past donations of tablets resulted in theft, breakage, and no support.

### What Success Looks Like for Mr. Mokoena
A teacher tells him about the app. He verifies: "Does it need internet? No. Does it cost money? No. Does it collect children's data? No. Do I need to install anything on the school computers? No." He approves its use. Six months later, it still works, he has received zero complaints, and he mentions it in his annual school improvement plan.

## 5. Dr. Ndaba — Content Creator / Curriculum Specialist

### Context & Background
Zanele Ndaba (39) has a PhD in Mathematics Education from Stellenbosch University. She works at a provincial education department as a subject advisor for Intermediate Phase Mathematics. She develops training materials for teachers and reviews supplementary learning resources for alignment with the CAPS curriculum. She has strong opinions about mathematical pedagogy — she believes in multiple representations (visual, symbolic, contextual) and culturally relevant examples.

### Device
- **Work laptop:** Dell Latitude (Windows 11), provided by the department.
- **Personal phone:** iPhone 13 (she is not a target user of the app itself, but she reviews content on Android test devices the project provides).

### Connectivity
- Office has reliable fibre internet.
- Home has uncapped Wi-Fi.
- She is not constrained by connectivity, but she understands that the learners she serves are.

### Goals
- Ensure the content in eZansiEdgeAI is mathematically rigorous and CAPS-aligned, term by term.
- Provide multiple explanation pathways: visual/diagrammatic, real-world context (using South African everyday scenarios), and procedural step-by-step.
- Maintain quality control over AI-generated explanations — nothing goes to learners without her sign-off.
- Author content packs efficiently using accessible tooling (Markdown, structured templates), not proprietary platforms.

### Pain Points
- Most ed-tech platforms use fixed content she can't edit or adapt.
- AI-generated maths explanations often contain subtle errors (incorrect worked examples, ambiguous notation) that only a subject expert catches.
- She has no way to test how content renders on a low-end phone without physically borrowing one.
- Existing content authoring tools (e.g., proprietary LMS editors) are clunky and don't support mathematical notation well.

### What Success Looks Like for Dr. Ndaba
She opens a structured Markdown template, writes an explanation for "comparing fractions using area models", adds a SVG diagram, tags it with the CAPS topic code and term, and exports it into a content-pack delta file. She previews it on an Android emulator configured to match the baseline device. She flags two AI-generated alternatives as mathematically unsound, approves three, and ships the update — all within a morning's work. Weeks later, a teacher in Limpopo tells her the fractions section "just makes sense."

## Persona Usage Guide

### During Design
- Walk every screen through Thandiwe's eyes: Can she use this on a cracked screen with no data?
- Walk every workflow through Ms. Dlamini's eyes: Can she do this in a 5-minute break between classes?
- Walk every deployment decision through Mr. Mokoena's eyes: Does this add cost or liability?

### During Development
- Test on Sipho's device class (mid-range) **and** Thandiwe's device class (low-end). If it doesn't work on hers, it doesn't ship.
- Ensure multi-profile support works for shared devices (Sipho and his sister).

### During Content Creation
- Dr. Ndaba is the quality gate. If the authoring workflow frustrates her, content quality will degrade.
- Every explanation must work at the reading level Sipho and Thandiwe need.

### During Deployment
- If Mr. Mokoena asks four yes/no questions and any answer is wrong, adoption fails at the school level.
- If Ms. Dlamini can't distribute the app in 15 minutes, it won't reach learners.
